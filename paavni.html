Introduction


Solar energy, derived from radiant light and heat emitted by the Sun, plays a pivotal role in advancing sustainable technologies. Its applications, ranging from solar power for electricity generation to solar thermal energy and innovative solar architecture, position it as a crucial source of renewable energy. The methodologies employed in harnessing solar energy can be broadly categorized into passive and active solar techniques, each with its distinct approach to capturing and utilizing this abundant resource.
Active solar techniques embrace cutting-edge technologies such as photovoltaic systems, concentrated solar power, and solar water heating. These methods directly convert solar energy into usable power, contributing to the diversification of energy sources and reducing dependence on conventional fuels. On the other hand, passive solar techniques leverage design principles, like strategic building orientation, the use of materials with favorable thermal mass, and the creation of naturally ventilated spaces. These approaches highlight the integration of solar energy considerations into architectural and environmental design, promoting energy efficiency and sustainability.
The significance of affordable and clean solar energy technologies was underscored by the International Energy Agency in 2011. The development of such technologies not only promises substantial long-term benefits but also addresses critical global challenges. By relying on an indigenous and inexhaustible resource, countries can enhance their energy security and reduce dependence on imported energy, fostering a more sustainable and resilient energy infrastructure. Furthermore, the adoption of solar energy contributes to the reduction of pollution and mitigates the impacts of global warming, aligning with global efforts to combat climate change.

Potential

The Earth, a recipient of a staggering 174 petawatts (PW) of incoming solar radiation at the upper atmosphere, experiences a dynamic interplay of energy. This immense influx, known as insolation, is a testament to the Sun's role as a boundless source of power for our planet of this formidable energy, around 30% is gracefully reflected back into space, creating a delicate cosmic ballet. The remaining 122 PW embarks on a transformative journey, with clouds, oceans, and terrestrial landscapes absorbing its radiance.nhabitants of our world find their homes in areas characterized by insolation levels ranging from 150 to 300 watts per square meter (W/m²), equivalent to 3.5 to 7.0 kilowatt-hours per square meter (kWh/m²) per day. This geographical diversity in solar exposure shapes the environmental conditions of regions where the majority of the global population resides. It is within this radiant spectrum that opportunities for harnessing solar energy abound, offering a renewable and sustainable pathway to meet the energy needs of diverse communities across the planet.

The intricate ballet of solar radiation unfolds as the Earth's land surface, encompassing diverse landscapes, and the vast expanses of oceans—enveloping approximately 71% of the globe—eagerly absorb the radiant energy from the Sun. This absorbed energy sets in motion a symphony of atmospheric processes, fueled by the transformative dance of warmth and water vapor. As the sun's energy permeates the Earth, warm air laden with evaporated water rises, instigating atmospheric circulation and the mesmerizing rhythm of convection.In the upper echelons of the atmosphere, where temperatures plummet, the airborne water vapor undergoes a breathtaking metamorphosis. Condensing into ethereal clouds, these moisture-laden formations embark on a celestial journey, releasing their accumulated droplets in a gentle raindance upon the Earth's surface. This act, a cornerstone of the water cycle, is a poetic interplay of nature's forces, sustaining life and ecosystems across the globe.

Beneath this cosmic theater, sunlight absorbed by the oceans and terrestrial landscapes plays a vital role in maintaining the Earth's surface at an average temperature of 14 °C. This delicate equilibrium sustains the conditions conducive to life as we know it. Adding another layer to this narrative, green plants, in a breathtaking display of biological alchemy known as photosynthesis, convert solar energy into chemically stored energy. This transformative process not only produces the sustenance that fuels life on Earth but also gives birth to the biomass from which essential fossil fuels are derived. In essence, the story of solar radiation and its terrestrial journey is a mesmerizing tale of interconnected forces, seamlessly weaving together the elements that sustain life, shape climates, and propel the intricate dance of our planet within the cosmic ballet of the solar system.

Earth annually absorbs a staggering 122 petawatts (PW) of solar energy, equivalent to 3,850,000 exajoules (EJ). In a striking revelation, the energy harnessed in just one hour and 25 minutes in 2002 (2019) surpassed the world's entire annual energy consumption. Photosynthesis, a botanical marvel, captures about 3,000 EJ per year, crafting biomass and underscoring the profound energy interplay sustaining life on our plan.


Unlocking the full potential of solar energy for human use is influenced by various factors, including geography, temporal fluctuations, cloud cover, and the land accessible to humans. In 2021, the Carbon Tracker Initiative provided a crucial insight, estimating that the land area required to generate the entirety of our energy from solar sources alone would be approximately 450,000 km2. This is comparable to the land area of Sweden, Morocco, or California, constituting a mere 0.3% of the Earth's total land area. This assessment underscores the importance of considering practical constraints and optimizing technological advancements to harness solar energy on a large scale."
Solar technologies exhibit a dual classification—passive or active—dictated by their methods of capturing, converting, and distributing sunlight. They facilitate the harnessing of solar energy across diverse global regions, a process often contingent on proximity to the equator. While solar energy primarily denotes the utilization of solar radiation for practical applications, it's noteworthy that, with the exception of Geothermal power and Tidal power, all renewable energy sources derive their energy, either directly or indirectly, from the Sun.
Active solar techniques employ a range of technologies, including photovoltaics, concentrated solar power, solar thermal collectors, pumps, and fans, to efficiently convert sunlight into practical outputs. In contrast, passive solar techniques involve strategic choices such as selecting materials with optimal thermal properties, designing spaces that naturally facilitate air circulation, and aligning buildings with the Sun's position. Notably, active solar technologies contribute to bolstering the energy supply, classifying them as supply-side technologies. On the other hand, passive solar technologies function by diminishing the reliance on alternative resources, positioning them as demand-side technologies, as they actively reduce the need for additional energy inputs.

Thermal Energy

Solar thermal technologies, harnessing the power of the sun's radiant energy, offer versatile applications encompassing water heating, space heating, space cooling, and the efficient generation of process heat. These innovative technologies provide sustainable solutions for a wide range of thermal needs, promoting environmental stewardship and reducing dependence on conventional energy sources.


Early Commercial Adaption

In 1878, at the Universal Exposition in Paris, Augustin Mouchot marked a pioneering moment in solar technology by successfully demonstrating a solar steam engine. Unfortunately, despite this early breakthrough, further development was hindered by the prevalence of cheap coal and other economic factors. Fast-forward to 1897, when Frank Shuman, an inventive engineer and solar energy trailblazer in the United States, took up the mantle. Shuman's innovative spirit materialized in a small-scale solar engine, where reflected solar energy heated square boxes filled with ether, powering a steam engine through internal black pipes. By 1908, Shuman founded the Sun Power Company with ambitions to construct larger solar power plants. Collaborating with technical advisor A.S.E. Ackermann and British physicist Sir Charles Vernon Boys, Shuman enhanced the system by incorporating mirrors to intensify solar energy on collector boxes, enabling the use of water instead of ether. This advancement culminated in the construction of a full-scale steam engine powered by low-pressure water, ultimately leading to the patenting of the entire solar engine system in 1912.

Shuman's groundbreaking contributions to solar energy reached their zenith when he successfully erected the world's first solar thermal power station in Maadi, Egypt, during the period spanning 1912 to 1913. This visionary plant employed parabolic troughs to harness solar energy, propelling a 45–52 kilowatts (60–70 hp) engine that efficiently pumped over 22,000 liters (4,800 imperial gallons; 5,800 US gallons) of water per minute from the Nile River to nearby cotton fields. Despite the disruptive influences of World War I and the subsequent era of inexpensive oil discoveries in the 1930s, which momentarily stifled the progress of solar energy, Shuman's foresight and fundamental design resurfaced in the 1970s. This renaissance marked a renewed fascination with solar thermal energy. Notably, in 1916, Shuman was a vocal advocate for the utilization of solar energy, emphasizing its significance in the media and laying the groundwork for the resurgence of interest in this sustainable power source.

We have proved the commercial profit of sun power in the tropics and have more particularly proved that after our stores of oil and coal are exhausted the human race can receive unlimited power from the rays of the Sun. — Frank Shuman, New York Times, 2 July 1916

Water Heating

Solar hot water systems harness sunlight to heat water, meeting 60-70% of domestic hot water needs in middle latitudes (40° N to 40° S), reaching temperatures of up to 60°C (140°F). Predominant types include evacuated tube collectors (44%) and glazed flat plate collectors (34%) for domestic use, and unglazed plastic collectors (21%) primarily employed in heating swimming pools.

As of 2015, global installed capacity for solar hot water systems reached around 436 thermal gigawatts (GWth), with China emerging as the dominant player, boasting 309 GWth and commanding a 71% market share. Remarkably, Israel and Cyprus lead per capita adoption, with over 90% of homes utilizing solar hot water systems. In the United States, Canada, and Australia, solar hot water finds its predominant use in heating swimming pools, contributing to an installed capacity of 18 GWth as of 2005.

Eating,Cooling and ventilation

In the United States, a substantial portion of energy consumption in buildings is attributed to heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems, comprising 30% (4.65 EJ/yr) in commercial buildings and nearly half, approximately 50% (10.1 EJ/yr), in residential buildings. Integrating solar heating, cooling, and ventilation technologies presents an opportunity to mitigate this energy demand. Solar heating strategies can be broadly categorized into passive solar concepts and active solar concepts, the latter involving elements such as sun tracking and solar concentrator optics for enhanced efficiency.
Thermal mass encompasses materials like stone, cement, and water, serving as a reservoir for storing solar heat. Traditionally utilized in arid or warm temperate regions, these materials absorb sunlight during the day, subsequently radiating stored heat to the cooler atmosphere at night to keep buildings cool. Notably, they also prove valuable in colder climates, aiding in maintaining warmth. The strategic sizing and placement of thermal mass, influenced by factors like climate, daylighting, and shading conditions, contribute to stabilizing space temperatures within a comfortable range. This integration minimizes reliance on auxiliary heating and cooling equipment, emphasizing the efficiency and versatility of thermal mass in enhancing building energy dynamics.
A solar chimney, also known as a thermal chimney in this context, functions as a passive solar ventilation system by incorporating a vertical shaft linking a building's interior and exterior. When the chimney is heated, the air inside warms, creating an upward airflow that effectively draws air through the building. Enhancements in performance can be achieved through strategic use of glazing and thermal mass materials, simulating the principles employed in greenhouses. This innovative design not only promotes natural ventilation but also optimizes energy efficiency by harnessing solar-induced airfl.
Deciduous trees and plants serve as a strategic tool for managing solar heating and cooling in building design. When positioned on the southern side in the northern hemisphere or the northern side in the southern hemisphere, their foliage provides vital shade in the summer, while the bare branches permit sunlight penetration during the winter. The delicate balance between the benefits of summer shading and the potential loss of winter heating is influenced by the fact that leafless trees can shade 1/3 to 1/2 of incident solar radiation. In regions with substantial heating needs, caution is advised against planting deciduous trees on the Equator-facing side of a building, as they may impede winter solar availability. However, they can be strategically placed on the east and west sides to offer effective summer shading without significantly compromising winter solar gain.

Cooking

Solar cookers harness sunlight for cooking, drying, and pasteurization, falling into three main categories: box cookers, panel cookers, and reflector cookers. The earliest solar cooker, pioneered by Horace de Saussure in 1767, is the box cooker—a basic, insulated container with a transparent lid. Remarkably, it remains effective even under partially overcast skies, reaching temperatures of 90–150 °C (194–302 °F). Panel cookers utilize reflective panels to direct sunlight onto an insulated container, achieving temperatures comparable to box cookers. Reflector cookers employ various concentrating geometries, such as dishes, troughs, and Fresnel mirrors, to focus light on a cooking container, attaining temperatures of 315 °C (599 °F) and beyond. However, these reflector cookers necessitate direct sunlight for optimal function and require repositioning to track the Sun.

Process Heat

Solar concentrating technologies, such as parabolic dish, trough, and Scheffler reflectors, offer a versatile solution for generating process heat in commercial and industrial settings. The pioneering Solar Total Energy Project (STEP) in Shenandoah, Georgia, marked a significant milestone by utilizing 114 parabolic dishes to supply 50% of the process heating, air conditioning, and electrical needs for a clothing factory. This grid-connected cogeneration system produced 400 kW of electricity along with thermal energy—401 kW in steam and 468 kW in chilled water—featuring a one-hour peak load thermal storage capacity. Beyond industrial applications, ,evaporation ponds represent an enduring use of solar energy, historically employed to extract salt from seawater. In contemporary contexts,evaporation ponds play a crucial role in concentrating brine solutions for leach mining and extracting dissolved solids from waste streams, showcasing the ongoing evolution and diversification of solar technologies in various sectors.
Clothes lines, clotheshorses, and clothes racks capitalize on wind and sunlight for clothes drying through evaporation, eliminating the need for electricity or gas consumption. In some U.S. states, legislation safeguards the "right to dry" clothes, promoting sustainable practices. Unglazed transpired collectors (UTC) function as perforated sun-facing walls, preheating ventilation air. These collectors elevate incoming air temperature by up to 22 °C (40 °F) and provide outlet temperatures ranging from 45–60 °C (113–140 °F). Notably, the swift payback period (3 to 12 years) positions transpired collectors as a cost-effective alternative to glazed systems. By 2003, over 80 systems, with a combined collector area of 35,000 square meters (380,000 sq ft), were installed globally. This included an 860 m2 (9,300 sq ft) collector in Costa Rica for drying coffee beans and a 1,300 m2 (14,000 sq ft) collector in Coimbatore, India, dedicated to drying marigolds. These installations underscore the worldwide adoption of transpired collectors across diverse applications.

Water Treatment

Solar distillation proves invaluable for converting saline or brackish water into potable water, a practice pioneered by 16th-century Arab alchemists. The first large-scale solar distillation project was implemented in 1872 in the Chilean mining town of Las Salinas. This groundbreaking plant featured a solar collection area of 4,700 m2 (51,000 sq ft), producing an impressive 22,700 L (5,000 imp gal; 6,000 US gal) per day and operating seamlessly for 40 years. Various solar still designs cater to different needs, including single-slope, double-slope (or greenhouse type), vertical, conical, inverted absorber, multi-wick, and multiple-effect configurations. These stills operate in passive, active, or hybrid modes. Notably, double-slope stills emerge as the most cost-effective option for decentralized domestic purposes, while active multiple effect units demonstrate greater suitability for large-scale applications. The versatility of solar distillation techniques underscores their effectiveness in addressing water scarcity and providing sustainable solutions for water purification.


Solar water disinfection (SODIS) utilizes sunlight to treat water-filled PET bottles, exposing them for several hours, with recommended exposure times ranging from six hours to two days in fully overcast conditions. Endorsed by the World Health Organization, SODIS is a recognized and widely adopted method for household water treatment and safe storage. Over two million people in developing countries rely on this cost-effective and accessible approach for their daily drinking water needs.
Solar energy proves environmentally friendly in water stabilization ponds, offering a chemical- and electricity-free method for wastewater treatment. Notably, these ponds foster the growth of algae, which play a dual role—consuming carbon dioxide through photosynthesis while aiding in waste breakdown. However, it's crucial to address potential challenges, as some algae species may release toxic chemicals that could render the water unusable. Despite this consideration, the overall impact is a sustainable and energy-efficient approach to wastewater treatment, showcasing the multifaceted benefits of solar energy in environmental stewardship.

Molten Salt Technology

Molten salt serves as a highly effective thermal energy storage method in concentrated solar power plants, preserving collected thermal energy from solar towers or troughs. This stored energy becomes invaluable for electricity generation during adverse weather conditions or at night. Demonstrated in the Solar Two project from 1995 to 1999, this system is anticipated to achieve an annual efficiency of 99%, emphasizing the importance of storing heat before converting it into electricity, as opposed to direct heat-to-electricity conversion. The molten salt mixture, often including sodium nitrate, potassium nitrate, and calcium nitrate, is non-flammable and non-toxic, drawing on proven experience from its use in chemical and metals industries as a heat-transport fluid. This establishes a reliable foundation for the application of such systems beyond solar contexts.
The molten salt employed in this system undergoes a phase change, melting at 131 °C (268 °F) and remaining in a liquid state at 288 °C (550 °F) within a well-insulated "cold" storage tank. The liquid salt is efficiently circulated through panels in a solar collector, where focused irradiance elevates its temperature to an impressive 566 °C (1,051 °F). Subsequently, the heated salt is directed to a hot storage tank, meticulously insulated to retain thermal energy effectively for up to a week. This robust and sophisticated process underscores the capacity of molten salt technology to store and utilize solar-generated thermal energy efficiently over extended periods, contributing to the reliability and stability of solar power plants.
Upon demand for electricity, the heated salt is pumped to a standard steam generator, where it undergoes the conventional process of producing superheated steam for a turbine/generator. This mechanism mirrors the operations seen in traditional coal, oil, or nuclear power plants. To power a 100-megawatt turbine for a four-hour duration, the storage tank required for this design would measure approximately 9.1 meters (30 ft) in height and 24 meters (79 ft) in diameter. This innovative integration of molten salt technology highlights its scalability and adaptability for large-scale power generation, contributing to the versatility of concentrated solar power systems.
Several parabolic trough power plants in Spain and SolarReserve, a solar power tower developer, have successfully implemented the thermal energy storage concept using molten salt. The Solana Generating Station in the U.S. showcases six hours of storage capacity through this technology. In Chile, the Cerro Dominador power plant features a 110 MW solar-thermal tower employing molten salts for heat transfer. The molten salts then pass their heat through a heat exchanger to water, generating superheated steam that powers a turbine, utilizing the Rankine cycle to convert steam's kinetic energy into electricity. The Cerro Dominador plant, with its advanced storage system, can generate stable electricity for up to 17.5 hours without direct solar radiation, ensuring uninterrupted power supply as needed. This innovative project, securing a sale of up to 950 GW·h per year, exemplifies the potential and reliability of molten salt technology. Another notable venture is the María Elena plant, a 400 MW thermo-solar complex in northern Chile, leveraging molten salt technology for efficient energy storage.

Electricity Production

Solar power entails the transformation of sunlight into electricity, achieved either directly through photovoltaics (PV) or indirectly via concentrated solar power. Photovoltaic cells employ the photovoltaic effect to convert light into an electric current. In concentrated solar power systems, lenses or mirrors, along with solar tracking systems, concentrate sunlight onto a specific focal point, typically used to drive a steam turbine. This diverse approach to harnessing solar energy underscores the adaptability and efficiency of solar power technologies.
Originally employed for small to medium-sized applications, from calculators to off-grid rooftop PV systems for remote homes, photovoltaics (PV) have evolved significantly. The commercial development of concentrated solar power plants emerged in the 1980s. Subsequently, with the decreasing cost of solar electricity, grid-connected solar PV systems have experienced exponential growth, approximately doubling every three years. The momentum has led to millions of installations and the construction of gigawatt-scale photovoltaic power stations. Remarkably, in 2021, half of the new generation capacity added was attributed to solar power, highlighting the pivotal role solar energy plays in the global energy landscape.
As of 2022, solar power accounted for 4.5% of the world's electricity generation, a significant increase from the 1% reported in 2015 when the Paris Agreement aimed at mitigating climate change was signed. Alongside onshore wind, utility-scale solar installations now boast the most cost-effective levelized cost of electricity in many countries. This shift underscores the remarkable progress and growing economic viability of solar energy, aligning with global efforts to transition to more sustainable and cleaner energy sources.
In 2022, nearly half of the newly installed solar power capacity was attributed to rooftop installations, reflecting a significant trend towards decentralized and distributed energy generation. Recognized as a crucial component in climate change mitigation, low-carbon power, including solar, has been advocated for its environmental benefits. However, the International Energy Agency emphasized the necessity for increased efforts in grid integration and addressing challenges related to policy, regulation, and financing. This highlights the importance of a comprehensive approach to advancing solar energy adoption and overcoming barriers to its seamless integration into existing energy infrastructures.

Concentrated Solar Power

Concentrating Solar Power (CSP) systems employ lenses, mirrors, and tracking systems to concentrate sunlight into a small beam, generating intense heat. This concentrated heat serves as a valuable heat source for conventional power plants. A variety of concentrating technologies, including parabolic troughs, solar tower collectors, concentrating linear Fresnel reflectors, and Stirling dishes, have been developed. Diverse techniques are utilized to track the Sun and focus light effectively. In these systems, a working fluid is heated by concentrated sunlight and subsequently employed for power generation or energy storage. However, the designs of CSP systems need to address potential risks such as dust storms, hail, or extreme weather events that may damage the delicate glass surfaces of solar power plants. To mitigate this risk, incorporating metal grills is a strategic solution—allowing a high percentage of sunlight to reach mirrors and solar panels while offering protection against potential damage. This thoughtful integration enhances the resilience and longevity of CSP systems in various environmental conditions..

Architecture and urban planning

Sunlight has exerted a profound influence on building design throughout architectural history. The Greeks and Chinese were pioneers in advanced solar architecture and urban planning, strategically orienting their structures toward the south to optimize exposure to light and warmth. Key features of passive solar architecture include the orientation relative to the Sun, a compact proportion (low surface area to volume ratio), selective shading (utilizing overhangs), and incorporating thermal mass. When tailored to local climate and environment, these features create well-lit spaces that maintain a comfortable temperature range. An exemplar of passive solar design is Socrates' Megaron House. Contemporary solar design approaches leverage computer modeling to integrate solar lighting, heating, and ventilation systems, creating a cohesive solar design package. Active solar equipment, such as pumps, fans, and switchable windows, complements passive design, enhancing overall system performance. This synergy of passive and active elements represents the latest strides in solar-informed building design, optimizing both efficiency and comfort.
Urban heat islands (UHI) characterize metropolitan areas with elevated temperatures compared to their surroundings. This phenomenon stems from increased absorption of solar energy by urban materials like asphalt and concrete, featuring lower albedos and higher heat capacities than natural surfaces. A simple yet effective strategy to counteract UHI is to paint buildings and roads white and introduce more trees to the area. In an illustrative example, a "cool communities" program in Los Angeles projects a potential reduction of urban temperatures by approximately 3 °C with an estimated cost of US$1 billion. The resulting benefits include an estimated annual total of US$530 million from reduced air-conditioning costs and healthcare savings, underscoring the economic and health advantages of mitigating UHI through thoughtful urban planning and design.

Agriculture and horticulture

In agriculture and horticulture, the optimization of solar energy capture plays a pivotal role in enhancing plant productivity. Various techniques, such as strategically timed planting cycles, tailored row orientation, staggered heights between rows, and the diverse mixing of plant varieties, are employed to maximize crop yields. Although sunlight is generally abundant, historical exceptions underscore its critical importance in agriculture. For instance, during the brief growing seasons of the Little Ice Age, farmers in France and England utilized fruit walls as thermal masses to accelerate ripening by maintaining warmth. Originally constructed perpendicular to the ground and facing south, these walls evolved over time with sloping designs for improved sunlight utilization. In 1699, Nicolas Fatio de Duillier proposed a solar tracking mechanism to pivot and follow the Sun. Beyond cultivating crops, solar energy finds applications in agriculture for tasks such as water pumping, crop drying, chick brooding, and chicken manure drying. This technology has also gained traction in viticulture, with vintners harnessing solar panel-generated energy to power grape presses, exemplifying its versatile applications across the agricultural spectrum.
Greenhouses serve as innovative structures that harness solar light, converting it into heat and enabling year-round production while facilitating the cultivation of specialty crops and plants that may not thrive in the local climate. The concept of greenhouses dates back to Roman times, where primitive structures were employed to produce cucumbers throughout the year for the Roman emperor Tiberius. However, it was during the 16th century in Europe that the first modern greenhouses emerged, primarily constructed to safeguard exotic plants brought back from overseas explorations. Today, greenhouses continue to play a crucial role in horticulture, providing controlled environments for optimal plant growth. The evolution of materials has expanded these practices, with plastic transparent materials finding application in polytunnels and row covers, replicating the greenhouse effect and extending the possibilities of year-round cultivation and protection of crops. This adaptation showcases the enduring significance and adaptability of greenhouse technology in meeting the challenges of diverse climates and crop requirements.

Transport

The pursuit of a solar-powered car has been an ambitious engineering endeavor since the 1980s, marked by significant milestones and competitions that highlight the progress in this field. A prominent event showcasing the evolution of solar-powered vehicles is the biannual World Solar Challenge, a race spanning 3,021 kilometers (1,877 miles) across central Australia from Darwin to Adelaide. Initiated in 1987, this competition draws teams from universities and enterprises worldwide. In its early years, the winning car boasted an average speed of 67 kilometers per hour (42 mph), and over the decades, advancements in solar vehicle technology have propelled the winner's average speed to an impressive 90.87 kilometers per hour (56.46 mph) by 2007. This remarkable improvement underscores the continuous innovation and dedication to enhancing the efficiency and speed of solar-powered cars. Reflecting international interest, the North American Solar Challenge and the upcoming South African Solar Challenge stand as comparable competitions, further emphasizing the global commitment to advancing the engineering and development of sustainable, solar-powered vehicles. These competitions serve as crucibles of innovation, driving progress towards the realization of a cleaner and more sustainable future for transportation.
Innovative automotive designs incorporate solar panels to harness the power of the sun, not just for propulsion but also for enhancing energy efficiency. Some vehicles seamlessly integrate solar panels to provide auxiliary power, specifically for functions like air conditioning. This forward-thinking approach not only keeps the interior cool without relying solely on traditional fuel sources but also contributes to a significant reduction in overall fuel consumption. By tapping into renewable solar energy, these vehicles exemplify a sustainable and eco-friendly solution, demonstrating the potential for cutting-edge technology to transform the driving experience while minimizing environmental impact.
In 1975, the first practical solar boat emerged in England. By 1995, passenger boats with integrated PV panels became common. Kenichi Horie achieved the first solar-powered Pacific Ocean crossing in 1996, and the Sun21 catamaran accomplished the inaugural solar-powered Atlantic Ocean crossing in 2006–2007. Plans for a global circumnavigation were set for 2010.
In 1974, the AstroFlight Sunrise marked the first solar flight, while the Solar Riser achieved the inaugural manned, solar-powered flight in 1979, reaching 40 ft (12 m) altitude. The Gossamer Penguin (1980) and Solar Challenger (1981) further advanced solar aviation, with the latter crossing the English Channel. In 1990, Eric Scott Raymond flew from California to North Carolina in 21 hops using solar power. Subsequent breakthroughs included the Pathfinder (1997) and the record-setting Helios (2001). BAE Systems' Zephyr set a 54-hour flight record in 2007, envisioning month-long flights by 2010. Notably, Solar Impulse, an electric aircraft, circled the globe from March 2015 to July 2016, showcasing solar-powered, long-duration flight capabilities.
A solar balloon, typically a black balloon filled with ordinary air, harnesses the transformative power of sunlight. When exposed to sunlight, the black surface absorbs solar radiation, heating the air inside and causing it to expand. This expansion generates an upward buoyancy force, akin to the principles behind artificially heated hot air balloons. Although some solar balloons are constructed on a scale large enough to accommodate human flight, their practical applications are mostly confined to the toy market. This limitation stems from the challenge of maintaining a favorable surface-area to payload-weight ratio, which becomes more pronounced as the size of the balloon increases. Nevertheless, these solar balloons serve as captivating demonstrations of solar heat's potential to provide lift and convey the fascinating interplay between solar energy and aerodynamics.

Fuel production

Solar chemical processes leverage solar energy to drive chemical reactions, offering a sustainable alternative to fossil fuel-derived energy. These processes not only offset reliance on traditional energy sources but also have the potential to transform solar energy into storable and transportable fuels. The two primary categories of solar-induced chemical reactions are thermochemical and photochemical. Artificial photosynthesis presents a promising avenue for producing various fuels, with the challenging multielectron catalytic chemistry involved in creating carbon-based fuels like methanol. Hydrogen production from protons offers a feasible alternative, with the potential for envisioning solar fuel plants in coastal metropolitan areas by 2050. These plants could utilize seawater, splitting it into hydrogen for fuel-cell electric power plants and producing pure water as a by-product for municipal water systems. Additionally, exploring chemical energy storage presents yet another avenue for addressing the challenges associated with solar energy storage, contributing to the overall sustainability and efficiency of solar technologies.
Hydrogen production technologies have been a focal point in solar chemical research since the 1970s. Beyond electrolysis driven by photovoltaic or photochemical cells, researchers have delved into various thermochemical processes. One innovative method involves concentrators to split water into oxygen and hydrogen at exceptionally high temperatures (2,300–2,600 °C or 4,200–4,700 °F). Another approach utilizes solar concentrators' heat to drive the steam reformation of natural gas, augmenting hydrogen yield compared to conventional methods. Thermochemical cycles, characterized by the decomposition and regeneration of reactants, offer a distinct avenue for hydrogen production. An example is the Solzinc process developed at the Weizmann Institute of Science, employing a 1 MW solar furnace to decompose zinc oxide (ZnO) at temperatures exceeding 1,200 °C (2,200 °F). This initial reaction yields pure zinc, which can then be reacted with water to produce hydrogen.

Energy storage methods

Thermal mass systems offer an effective means of storing solar energy in the form of heat, maintaining domestically useful temperatures for both daily and interseasonal durations. These systems leverage readily available materials with high specific heat capacities, such as water, earth, and stone. Ingeniously designed, they contribute to mitigating peak demand, optimizing time-of-use by shifting to off-peak hours, and diminishing overall heating and cooling requirements. By harnessing the inherent properties of these materials, thermal mass systems play a pivotal role in enhancing energy efficiency, promoting sustainable practices, and providing a reliable solution for storing and utilizing solar-generated heat in diverse climates and applications.
Phase change materials, such as paraffin wax and Glauber's salt, represent another innovative thermal storage medium for solar energy. These materials, characterized by their cost-effectiveness and ready availability, can maintain domestically useful temperatures, around 64 °C or 147 °F. Pioneering applications like the 'Dover House' in 1948 utilized Glauber's salt heating systems, showcasing the early adoption of these materials. In addition, solar energy can be stored at high temperatures through the use of molten salts. This method leverages the advantages of salts as a storage medium—being low-cost, possessing a high specific heat capacity, and capable of delivering heat at temperatures compatible with conventional power systems. A notable example is the Solar Two project, which harnessed molten salts to store 1.44 terajoules (400,000 kWh) in its 68 m³ storage tank, achieving an annual storage efficiency of about 99%. This demonstrates the viability of molten salts as a robust and efficient solution for high-temperature solar energy storage, offering a crucial component for advancing sustainable and scalable solar technologies
Off-grid photovoltaic (PV) systems have traditionally relied on rechargeable batteries to store surplus electricity. In contrast, grid-tied systems offer the flexibility of feeding excess electricity into the transmission grid, while drawing power from the grid during shortfalls. Net metering programs play a pivotal role in this dynamic by crediting household systems for the electricity they contribute to the grid. This is achieved through a 'rolling back' mechanism on the meter, effectively subtracting the surplus electricity produced from the total consumption. If the net electricity use falls below zero, the utility then carries over the kilowatt-hour credit to the following month. While some systems employ two meters to separately measure electricity consumed and produced, this approach is less common due to the added installation cost. Most standard meters are now equipped to accurately measure electricity flow in both directions, eliminating the need for a second meter. This streamlined approach not only simplifies the monitoring process but also contributes to the cost-effectiveness and widespread adoption of grid-tied PV systems with net metering
Pumped-storage hydroelectricity serves as a dynamic energy storage solution, capitalizing on the gravitational potential energy of water. During periods of surplus energy availability, water is pumped from a lower elevation reservoir to a higher elevation one, effectively storing the energy. When energy demand peaks, this stored energy is harnessed by releasing the water through turbines, transforming the pump into a hydroelectric power generator. This ingenious process not only facilitates the efficient management of fluctuating energy supply and demand but also underscores the adaptability and reliability of pumped-storage hydroelectricity as a crucial component in the broader landscape of renewable energy systems

Development, deployment and economics

Commencing with the surge in coal use during the Industrial Revolution, the trajectory of energy consumption witnessed a notable shift from wood and biomass to fossil fuels. The nascent phase of solar technology development in the 1860s was motivated by the anticipation of an imminent scarcity of coal. However, progress in solar technologies experienced a period of stagnation in the early 20th century, primarily due to the escalating availability, economic viability, and versatility of coal and petroleum. The dominance of these fossil fuels temporarily overshadowed the further advancement of solar technologies, redirecting focus towards the seemingly abundant and cost-effective energy sources of the time
The 1973 oil embargo and the subsequent 1979 energy crisis triggered a global restructuring of energy policies, prompting a renewed emphasis on the development of solar technologies. In response to these challenges, nations worldwide redirected their focus towards sustainable energy solutions. This era witnessed the implementation of strategic deployment strategies, with a particular emphasis on incentive programs. In the United States, the Federal Photovoltaic Utilization Program was established, while Japan initiated the Sunshine Program. Simultaneously, research facilities dedicated to solar energy emerged, such as the Solar Energy Research Institute (SERI), now known as the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) in the U.S., the New Energy and Industrial Technology Development Organization (NEDO) in Japan, and the Fraunhofer Institute for Solar Energy Systems ISE in Germany. These initiatives marked pivotal milestones in fostering international collaboration and advancing solar technologies as viable solutions in the global energy landscape.
Commercial solar water heaters made their debut in the United States during the 1890s and experienced growing adoption until the 1920s. However, the ascent of these systems was gradually eclipsed by the emergence of cheaper and more reliable heating fuels. Following the oil crises of the 1970s, interest in solar water heating was rekindled, only to wane again in the 1980s with declining petroleum prices. Nevertheless, the sector experienced a resurgence in development during the 1990s, witnessing steady progress. Since 1999, annual growth rates have consistently averaged 20%, indicating a sustained momentum in the evolution of solar water heating technologies. Despite being occasionally underestimated, solar water heating and cooling have emerged as the most widely deployed solar technology, boasting an estimated capacity of 154 GW as of 2007

The advancement of affordable, inexhaustible, and clean solar energy technologies holds immense long-term advantages. Beyond contributing to global energy security by relying on indigenous, renewable, and mostly import-independent resources, it serves as a cornerstone for sustainability, pollution reduction, and cost-effective mitigation of climate change. By fostering widespread adoption, solar energy not only mitigates environmental impact but also helps maintain lower fossil fuel prices. Recognizing these benefits as global imperatives, the additional costs incurred in incentivizing early deployment should be viewed as strategic learning investments. Ensuring their prudent allocation and widespread distribution is essential, as it not only accelerates the transition to sustainable energy but also ensures equitable access to the advantages of solar technology on a global scale.
In 2011, a groundbreaking report from the International Energy Agency unveiled the transformative potential of solar energy technologies, including photovoltaics, solar hot water, and concentrated solar power. The report indicated that, with unwavering political commitment to climate change mitigation and renewable energy transition, solar technologies could supply one-third of the world's energy by 2060. The sun's energy, harnessed through diverse applications ranging from small to large scale, emerges as a linchpin in decarbonizing the global economy. This shift, coupled with advancements in energy efficiency and imposing costs on greenhouse gas emitters, positions solar power as a multifaceted and pivotal force in steering the world towards a sustainable and low-carbon energy future. The versatility and adaptability of solar applications underscore its strength in reshaping our energy landscape.
In 2021, Lazard's assessment revealed a compelling economic landscape for solar energy, estimating the levelized cost of new, unsubsidized utility-scale solar electricity at less than $37 per megawatt-hour (MWh). This favorable cost comparison further underscored the economic viability of solar, as it outperformed the existing cost of coal-fired power. Moreover, the report highlighted that new solar projects were not only more cost-effective than new gas-fired power but generally competitive with existing gas power. This economic advantage positions solar as not only an environmentally sustainable choice but also a financially attractive and competitive option in the contemporary energy market, signaling a pivotal shift in the broader energy paradigm.

Emerging technologies

Experimental solar power

Concentrated photovoltaics (CPV) systems represent an innovative approach harnessing sunlight by concentrating it onto photovoltaic surfaces to generate electricity efficiently. In these systems, the focused sunlight enhances the performance of photovoltaic cells, optimizing electricity production. Complementing this, thermoelectric devices, also known as 'thermovoltaic' devices, leverage temperature differentials between dissimilar materials to generate an electric current. This intriguing technology taps into the principles of thermoelectricity, providing a direct and effective means of converting heat into electrical power. Together, concentrated photovoltaics and thermoelectric devices exemplify diverse strategies in the pursuit of sustainable and efficient energy generation from renewable sources

Floatimg solar arrays

Floating solar, also known as floating photovoltaics (FPV) or floatovoltaics, represents an innovative approach where solar panels are mounted on structures that float on bodies of water, including reservoirs, lakes, drinking water reservoirs, quarry lakes, irrigation canals, and remediation and tailing ponds. This sustainable technology is gaining momentum globally, with increasing implementations in countries such as China, France, India, Japan, South Korea, the United Kingdom, Singapore, and the United States. By utilizing water surfaces for solar installations,floating solar not only optimizes land use but also offers unique benefits, such as reduced evaporation in water reservoirs and increased energy efficiency through natural cooling effects. This approach underscores the versatility of solar energy applications and its potential to address both energy needs and environmental considerations in diverse regions worldwide.
Floating photovoltaic (FPV) systems offer distinct advantages over traditional land-based photovoltaics (PV). Water surfaces, as platforms for FPV installations, often present a more cost-effective alternative to land, and regulatory constraints are typically less stringent for structures on bodies of water not designated for recreational use. Additionally, life cycle analysis reveals that foam-based FPV systems exhibit remarkable environmental efficiency, boasting one of the shortest energy payback times at 1.3 years and the lowest greenhouse gas emissions to energy ratio (11 kg CO2 eq/MWh) among reported crystalline silicon solar photovoltaic technologies. This underscores the potential of FPV as a sustainable and economically viable solution in the realm of solar energy generation.
In contrast to numerous land-based solar plants, floating arrays possess a unique advantage in their unobtrusiveness, as they are discreetly concealed from public view. This characteristic not only addresses aesthetic concerns but also mitigates potential opposition to solar installations. Moreover, the efficiency of floating arrays surpasses that of their land-based counterparts, as the presence of water facilitates superior cooling for the panels. This enhanced cooling capability not only optimizes energy production but also contributes to the longevity of the panels by minimizing heat-induced wear and tear. Additionally, the implementation of special coatings on the panels serves as a protective measure, preventing rust and corrosion and further bolstering the resilience and durability of floating photovoltaic systems. This combination of visual inconspicuousness, heightened efficiency, and advanced protective features positions floating arrays as a promising and sustainable advancement in solar energy technology.
The trajectory of the renewable energy landscape has witnessed a remarkable surge in the market for floating solar technology, experiencing rapid growth since 2016. In its initial stages, from 2007 to 2013, the construction of the first 20 plants, each with capacities of a few dozen kWp, set the foundation for subsequent advancements. The momentum intensified, leading to a substantial increase in installed power, escalating from 3 GW in 2020 to an impressive 13 GW by 2022. This achievement not only surpassed the earlier prediction of reaching 10 GW by 2025 but also underscored the accelerated adoption and acceptance of floating solar solutions on a global scale. The World Bank's estimation further highlights the vast potential of this technology, identifying 6,600 large bodies of water deemed suitable for floating solar applications. With a technical capacity exceeding 4,000 GW if a modest 10% of their surfaces were covered with solar panels, these figures underscore the considerable untapped opportunities for expanding the reach and impact of floating solar on a truly massive scale.

Solar-assisted heat pump

A heat pump, a device engineered to transfer heat energy from a source to a designated "heat sink," plays a pivotal role in reshaping the dynamics of thermal energy transfer. Its innovative design facilitates the movement of thermal energy against the spontaneous heat flow, extracting heat from a colder space and delivering it to a warmer destination. The integration of a heat pump with thermal solar panels into a singular, cohesive system marks the advent of a solar-assisted heat pump. Unlike conventional approaches where these technologies operate independently or in parallel for hot water production, this integrated system strategically leverages solar thermal panels as the low-temperature heat source. In this innovative configuration, the solar thermal panel fulfills the crucial role of feeding the heat pump's evaporator, aiming to achieve a high Coefficient of Performance (COP). The overarching objective is to enhance energy production efficiency while concurrently reducing costs, thereby exemplifying a synergistic and economically viable approach to harnessing renewable energy.
The versatility of solar-assisted heat pump systems extends to the compatibility with various types of solar thermal panels, including sheet and tubes, roll-bond, heat pipe, and thermal plates, as well as hybrid configurations such as mono/polycrystalline and thin film. However, the preference for employing a hybrid panel becomes evident, as it introduces a multifaceted advantage. A hybrid panel not only serves the traditional purpose of providing thermal energy to the heat pump but also offers the distinct capability to partially cover the electricity demand of the heat pump. This dual functionality translates into a notable reduction in power consumption, consequently diminishing the variable costs associated with the entire system. Embracing a hybrid approach exemplifies an intelligent synergy between diverse solar technologies and the heat pump, presenting a holistic solution that not only maximizes energy efficiency but also contributes to the economic sustainability of the integrated system.

Solar aircraft

An electric aircraft represents a groundbreaking shift in aviation technology, departing from traditional internal combustion engines to embrace electric motors as its primary propulsion system. This transformative approach to aviation is made possible through various sources of electricity, including fuel cells, solar cells, ultracapacitors, power beaming, or advanced battery systems. Unlike conventional aircraft, electric planes leverage the efficiency and environmental benefits of electric propulsion, contributing to reduced carbon emissions and operational sustainability. The diverse range of power sources underscores the adaptability of electric aircraft, allowing for innovation in both energy generation and storage technologies. This evolution in aviation not only promises enhanced efficiency and performance but also aligns with the global commitment to sustainable and eco-friendly transportation solutions.
As of the present, manned electric aircraft predominantly exist in the realm of experimental demonstrators, highlighting the ongoing exploration and innovation within the aviation industry. Although batteries power numerous small unmanned aerial vehicles, the concept of electrically propelled model aircraft has been in existence since the 1970s, as evidenced by a report dating back to 1957. Pioneering a significant milestone, the first man-carrying electrically powered flights were achieved in 1973, marking a crucial step toward the realization of electric aviation. Notably, between 2015 and 2016, the Solar Impulse 2, a manned solar-powered plane, showcased the potential of sustainable aviation by successfully completing a historic circumnavigation of the Earth. This remarkable achievement not only demonstrated the viability of solar energy in aviation but also symbolized a pivotal moment in the transition towards cleaner and more energy-efficient modes of air travel.
